What is a blockchain?
The foundation of everything Paxos does
the simple version

A shared record book nobody controls

A blockchain is a permanent, tamper-proof ledger of transactions — copied across thousands of computers simultaneously. Once written, it cannot be erased or changed. Every transaction is timestamped and linked to the one before it.

The analogy: a blockchain is like a Google Doc that everyone can read, that auto-saves every change, and that nobody can delete — except instead of one server, it lives on thousands of computers at once.
the key properties

Why it matters

Decentralized: No single company or government controls it. To alter a record you would have to change every copy simultaneously — practically impossible.

Transparent: Every transaction is visible and auditable in real time. Regulators, auditors, and partners can verify history instantly.

Programmable (on most chains): Smart contracts — self-executing code — can attach rules to money. Pay when conditions are met. No human required.

Fast and cheap: Transactions that take days through banks take seconds on blockchain, for fractions of a cent.

bitcoin vs ethereum vs stellar

Not all blockchains are the same

Bitcoin's blockchain does one thing — records bitcoin ownership. It is a ledger only. Not programmable. You cannot build apps on it or issue stablecoins.

Ethereum is programmable — a platform where developers build applications. This is where Paxos issues most stablecoins. Think of Bitcoin as a calculator and Ethereum as a smartphone.

Stellar was built for payments and financial inclusion — fast, cheap, designed for the unbanked. This is what Civitas ID uses. Paxos expanded to Stellar in late 2024.

Your Civitas ID work on Stellar is directly relevant to Paxos. You understand the ecosystem from the inside. Use it.

Stablecoin
Paxos's core product — and the fastest growing segment of digital finance
what it is

A cryptocurrency always worth exactly $1

Most crypto goes up and down wildly — Bitcoin can drop 30% in a week. A stablecoin is pegged to a stable asset, usually the US dollar. One PYUSD always equals one dollar. The stability is the point.

The analogy: a stablecoin is like a traveller's check. The bank holds your real dollars. You get an instrument representing exactly that amount. Except this one moves at internet speed, 24/7, globally.

Three types — only one is safe at scale

Fiat-backed (what Paxos does): Real dollars in a real bank. For every token issued, one dollar is held in reserve. Fully backed. Safest model.

Crypto-backed: Backed by another cryptocurrency. Requires over-collateralization because crypto is volatile. Riskier.

Algorithmic (the dangerous one): No real reserve. A computer algorithm tries to maintain the peg through supply manipulation. This is what TerraLuna was. It collapsed catastrophically in 2022, losing $40 billion in 72 hours.

Paxos's four assets

PYUSD (PayPal USD): Paxos issues it, PayPal distributes it to 400 million users. $2.9 billion in circulation. Their most visible product.

USDG (Global Dollar): Paxos's partner network stablecoin. Partners share in the yield from reserves — a revenue incentive to distribute it.

USDP (Pax Dollar): Original Paxos stablecoin from 2018. Supply shrunk to $32M as Paxos shifted to partner-issued tokens.

PAXG (Pax Gold): Not a dollar stablecoin — pegged to gold. Each token equals one troy ounce of a real gold bar in a London vault. $2.6 billion market cap.

case study — catastrophic failure

TerraLuna — why reserve backing matters

TerraLuna was an algorithmic stablecoin — no real dollar reserves. In May 2022 a large holder sold heavily, breaking confidence in the peg. The algorithm tried to defend it by printing more tokens, making things worse. Within 72 hours $40 billion evaporated. The stablecoin went from $1 to near zero.

Millions of retail investors were wiped out. It triggered a broader crypto market crash and is the primary reason regulators now require full reserve backing.

Paxos was completely unaffected. Every token is backed by real dollars. This is their core safety proposition and a major reason institutional buyers choose them.

DeFi — Decentralized Finance
Financial services that run on code instead of banks
what it is

Banking without a bank

DeFi is the ecosystem of financial applications — lending, borrowing, earning interest, trading — that run on blockchains using smart contracts instead of banks or intermediaries.

The analogy: a bank decides whether to lend you money based on your credit score and a loan officer's judgment. A DeFi protocol lends automatically to anyone who deposits enough collateral — no application, no approval, no hours of operation. The code executes the rules.

How it works in plain language

Lending: Deposit stablecoins into a protocol like Aave. The protocol lends them to borrowers who post collateral. You earn interest automatically.

Borrowing: Post $150 of Ethereum as collateral. Borrow $100 in stablecoins. No credit check. No approval. If your collateral drops too much, the protocol automatically liquidates it.

Trading: Swap one cryptocurrency for another on a decentralized exchange like Uniswap. No company in the middle. A smart contract handles it instantly.

Paxos Labs connection

Why Paxos cares about DeFi now

Paxos's enterprise clients hold stablecoins but want to put them to work — earn yield, borrow against them. Paxos Labs is building the Amplify Suite to let companies access DeFi yield without building the blockchain infrastructure themselves. This is the "what now?" answer for stablecoin holders.

the risks

Why DeFi is risky

Smart contract bugs can be exploited — hackers have stolen billions from DeFi protocols. There is no FDIC insurance. No customer service. No safety nets. DeFi is powerful but operates entirely without the consumer protections traditional finance provides.

Wallet
How people hold and move crypto — and why Paxos paid $100M for Fordefi
what it is

A wallet holds your keys — not your coins

A crypto wallet does not hold coins the way a physical wallet holds cash. Your cryptocurrency lives on the blockchain. The wallet holds your private keys — the cryptographic password that proves you own those coins and authorizes transactions.

The analogy: your coins are in a safe deposit box (the blockchain). Your wallet is the key. Lose the key and you cannot access the box. Someone steals the key and they own everything in it.

Hot vs cold — custodial vs non-custodial

Hot wallet: Connected to the internet. Convenient for daily use but more exposed to hacking. MetaMask, Coinbase Wallet.

Cold wallet: Not connected to the internet. Like a safe. Ledger and Trezor are physical hardware cold wallets — they look like USB drives.

Custodial: Someone else holds your keys — you trust them (like a bank). They can freeze your account.

Non-custodial: You hold your own keys. Full control, full responsibility. No one can freeze your account. No one can help you if you lose access.

Private keys — the most important security concept in crypto

Every wallet has a public key (like your account number — safe to share) and a private key (like your password — never share it). Anyone who has your private key controls your funds completely.

Most wallets give you a seed phrase — 12 or 24 random words representing your private key. Write it on paper. Store it safely. Lose it and your funds are gone forever if your device breaks. Someone finds it and your funds are gone immediately.

Famous example: A man threw away a hard drive containing the private key to 7,500 Bitcoin — now worth hundreds of millions of dollars. He has been trying to get permission to dig up a UK landfill ever since.

Tokenization
Putting real-world assets on a blockchain — $180B and counting
what it is

A digital twin of any real-world asset

Tokenization means creating a blockchain token that represents ownership of a real asset — a dollar, a share of stock, a gold bar, a building, a piece of art. The token travels at blockchain speed. The asset stays where it is.

The analogy: a stock certificate proves you own shares. It is paper. Now imagine that paper is replaced by a digital token that transfers anywhere in the world in seconds, automatically, with no middlemen. That is tokenization.

What gets tokenized

Currency (stablecoins): Paxos's core business. A dollar token is a tokenized dollar.

Gold (PAXG): Each PAXG token equals one troy ounce of a specific gold bar in a London vault. Fully redeemable for physical metal.

Real estate: A $10M building tokenized into 10 million $1 tokens. Anyone can buy a fractional stake.

Identities (Civitas ID): Government-issued credentials tokenized as verifiable digital documents a person owns and controls. Your exact work.

Paxos has processed over $180 billion in tokenization activity. That number is the headline in every pitch to a bank or regulator.

Settlement in seconds instead of days

Trust Charter
Paxos's single most important competitive differentiator
what it is

A banking license for digital assets

A trust charter is a license from a banking regulator authorizing a company to hold customer assets in trust. It comes with strict capital requirements, regular audits, and ongoing supervision. One of the hardest licenses to obtain in financial services.

The analogy: the difference between a licensed pharmacist and someone selling pills from a backpack. Both might have the same medicine. Only one is authorized, supervised, and legally accountable. Banks will only work with the pharmacist.

Paxos holds four regulatory licenses

NYDFS Trust Charter (2015): First crypto company to receive one. The New York Department of Financial Services supervises all Paxos operations. This is why banks can partner with them.

OCC National Trust Charter: Federal-level oversight from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency. Even more significant — federal banking regulation, not just state level.

MAS License (Singapore): Major Payment Institution license. Allows global issuance of USDG through Singapore entity.

MiCA (EU): EU stablecoin regulation compliance through their Finnish entity. Covers all 27 EU member states.

Why this is Paxos's competitive moat

Tether — the largest stablecoin at $100+ billion — has no US banking license and does not fully disclose its reserves. Banks will not touch it.

Paxos's trust charter means customer funds are held in bankruptcy-remote accounts — if Paxos fails, your money is legally separate and protected. Reserves are independently audited monthly. Every major operation is supervised.

PayPal chose Paxos not because they are cheap — because they are the only regulated option a publicly traded company could work with.

benefit 1

Eliminates the middlemen tax

Every time money moves through banks, correspondent banks, wire systems, clearinghouses — each takes a cut. A worker sending $200 home to Mexico might lose $15–$20 in fees. On blockchain that same transfer costs a fraction of a cent and arrives in seconds. At scale this represents hundreds of billions returned to people and businesses annually.

The analogy: the internet made information free to move. Blockchain makes money free to move.
benefit 2

Financial inclusion for 1.4 billion unbanked people

1.4 billion adults have no bank account. They cannot save safely, access credit, or receive payments. But over 5 billion people have smartphones. Blockchain lets anyone with a phone hold, send, and receive money — no bank required. This is the mission of Civitas ID and why Stellar was built.

benefit 3

Settlement in seconds frees trillions in capital

When stocks settle in two days, that money is frozen. Banks hold enormous reserves just to cover this gap. Tokenized stocks settling in seconds would free trillions of dollars of trapped capital. The Fed and every major bank are studying this.

benefit 4

Programmable money creates new economic models

Smart contracts let money execute automatically — pay a supplier the moment goods arrive, distribute royalties to 10,000 artists the second their song plays, send aid that can only be spent on school fees. Money that follows rules without human intervention is a fundamentally new tool.

benefit 5

Transparency reduces fraud and corruption

Every blockchain transaction is visible and permanent. Aid organizations can track whether funds reach recipients. Supply chains can prove provenance. In countries with corrupt banking systems, blockchain can make government payments auditable by citizens in real time.

The Dangers

danger 1

Volatility and speculation

Bitcoin fell 80% between 2021 and 2022. People who borrowed to invest lost everything. This is why stablecoins matter — they remove volatility while keeping the benefits of blockchain.

Paxos response: every PYUSD is always worth exactly $1. Fully backed. Fully regulated.
danger 2

Fraud, scams, and irreversibility

Send bitcoin to the wrong address and it is gone forever. No customer service. No fraud department. No chargebacks. The same permanence that makes blockchain trustworthy makes mistakes catastrophic. Crypto scams have cost consumers billions annually.

The analogy: blockchain is like cash in an envelope. Wrong address — no getting it back.
danger 3

Regulatory risk and legal uncertainty

Rules vary by country and change constantly. A company can be fully legal in one jurisdiction and illegal in another. This is why the GENIUS Act matters — clear federal rules reduce risk. And why Paxos's multi-jurisdiction licensing is a genuine moat.

danger 4

Stablecoin de-pegging — the TerraLuna lesson

TerraLuna collapsed from $1 to near zero in 72 hours in 2022, wiping out $40 billion. It was algorithmic — no real reserve. This is why Paxos's fully backed, monthly audited model is so important. The reserves are real. They proved it when they wound down BUSD and every holder redeemed at exactly $1.

danger 5

Money laundering and illicit use

Crypto has been used by bad actors to move money without detection. Regulators worry about sanctions evasion and ransomware payments. Paxos's response: full KYC/AML on all clients. They are not a workaround — they are the compliance layer.

danger 6

Environmental concern

Bitcoin uses enormous amounts of electricity — comparable to some countries. However, most newer blockchains including Ethereum (since 2022), Solana, and Stellar use proof-of-stake which uses 99.9% less energy. This distinction matters.
